Employers don't have time to analyze complex fee structures or understand what constitutes reasonable fees for plans their size. Interpreting plan benchmarking data and conducting meaningful comparisons requires specialized ERISA knowledge.

Infrequent benchmarking

What is a reasonable 401(k) fee?

Reasonable fees depend on plan size, services provided, and participant count. Industry data shows average total plan costs of 1.09% for small plans ($5M in plan assets, 50 participants), 0.76% for medium plans ($50M assets), and under 0.50% for large plans ($500M+ assets). Plans paying above these industry averages, especially those above 0.30% in administration fees alone, are likely overpaying. How do hidden 401(k) fees work and why are they hard to find?

Hidden fees, also called indirect compensation, include revenue sharing where mutual fund companies pay your recordkeeper from plan investments. These payments don't appear on invoices but reduce your investment returns through higher fund expense ratios. 12b-1 fees, sub-transfer agency fees, and wrap fees all function similarly. They're disclosed in your 408(b)(2) statement but calculating their dollar impact on plan assets requires expertise. We identify all hidden compensation and include it in your all-in fee calculation.

What's the difference between Live Bid benchmarking and database comparisons?

Database benchmarking compares your plan fees to industry averages, often using outdated Form 5500 data that misses indirect investment expenses. Our Live Bid approach obtains actual competitive proposals from qualified providers using your specific plan characteristics—participant count, plan assets, contribution patterns, and service requirements. This produces accurate, current pricing you can act on to determine if you're paying reasonable fees, not generic averages. The difference is similar to getting a firm quote versus checking industry standards.

What key metrics should plan fiduciaries track between benchmarking cycles?

Key metrics include total plan cost as a percentage of plan assets, average fund fees across your investment lineup, investment performance against appropriate benchmarks, and participant utilization rates. Track annual changes in administration fees and fund expense ratios, as these should decline over time with asset growth and industry fee compression. We help employers identify the right key metrics for their plan size and complexity, and our reports make it easy to monitor these between formal benchmarking cycles.

How does plan design affect fee benchmarking results?

Plan design significantly impacts costs. Plans with profit sharing, complex vesting schedules, or frequent loan processing incur higher administrative fees than simpler designs. Your plan's investment options—the number of funds, whether you offer brokerage windows, and average fund fees—affect total investment expenses. During benchmarking, we evaluate whether plan design features deliver value to justify their costs, helping you optimize the balance between robust benefits for employees and reasonable fees.
