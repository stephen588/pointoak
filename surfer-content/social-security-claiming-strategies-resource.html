Social Security Claiming Strategies: How to Maximize Your Retirement Benefits

Deciding when to claim Social Security benefits is one of the most consequential financial decisions you'll make in retirement. The difference between filing at age 62 versus waiting until age 70 can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars in lifetime benefits, yet more than two-thirds of workers still claim before reaching full retirement age. For plan sponsors helping employees navigate this decision, and for individuals approaching retirement themselves, understanding social security claiming strategies is essential to building a secure financial future.

Why Your Social Security Claiming Age Matters

Your monthly benefit amount changes significantly depending on when you start benefits. The Social Security Administration calculates your primary insurance amount (PIA) based on your earnings history, specifically your highest 35 years of annual earnings. That PIA represents what you'd receive at your full retirement age (FRA). For anyone born in 1960 or later, your full retirement age is 67 (your normal retirement age under current law), but you can claim as early as age 62 or delay as late as age 70.

The financial impact is substantial. Filing at age 62 permanently reduces your social security benefits by as much as 30%, dropping a $2,000 monthly benefit at full retirement age FRA to roughly $1,400 per month. Waiting until age 70 earns you delayed retirement credits of 8% per year beyond your FRA, boosting that same benefit to approximately $2,480, a 24% increase over your full social security benefits.

The annual earnings limit adds another layer. If you collect benefits before reaching full retirement age and continue working, the Social Security Administration reduces your benefit payments by $1 for every $2 earned above $24,480 in 2026.

Understanding Full Retirement Age and Benefit Calculations

Your full retirement age determines the baseline for every claiming decision. The benefit calculation starts with your earnings record, averaging your 35 highest-earning years to determine your primary insurance amount. If you have fewer than 35 years of covered employment, zeros fill the gaps, pulling your benefit amount down.

Your financial situation and life expectancy should drive the timing decision. Someone with a shorter life expectancy or immediate financial burden may benefit from claiming early. However, research consistently shows that waiting until age 70 helps maximize lifetime benefits for individuals who live to average life expectancy or beyond. Each month you delay past your FRA, your benefit grows by two-thirds of 1%, and those delayed retirement credits compound into significantly higher monthly payments for the rest of your life.

Once you reach your full retirement age, you unlock your full retirement benefits without any reduction. The break-even point typically falls around age 78 to 80. Beyond that point, the person who waited receives more money in total benefit payments.

Spousal Benefits and Survivor Benefits: Strategies for Married Couples

Social security income planning becomes more complex for married couples because spousal benefits and survivor benefits create additional claiming opportunities.

How Spousal Benefits Work

A spouse can claim spousal benefits worth up to 50% of the higher earning spouse's PIA, even if the lower earning spouse has limited or no earnings history of their own. To claim spousal benefits, the primary worker must have already filed for their own retirement benefit. If you're eligible for both your own benefits and a spousal benefit, you'll receive the higher benefit amount.

Since the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015 eliminated the "file and suspend" strategy, deemed filing rules now require most people to file for all benefits they're eligible for simultaneously. You can no longer claim spousal benefits while letting your own retirement benefit grow. However, survivor benefits are treated differently.

Survivor Benefits for a Surviving Spouse

When one spouse passes away, the surviving spouse receives the higher of the two social security payments coming into the household. This is why it often makes sense for the higher earner to delay claiming as long as possible: it locks in a larger benefit that protects the surviving spouse financially. The surviving spouse receives up to 100% of the deceased worker's benefit if they wait until their own full retirement age to claim survivor benefits based on that record.

A key strategy for married couples: the lower earning spouse claims their own benefits early to provide household income, while the higher earning spouse delays until age 70 to maximize the eventual survivor benefit. This approach balances current cash flow needs against long-term protection.

Divorced Spousal Benefits

If your marriage lasted at least 10 years and you haven't remarried, you may qualify for a divorced spousal benefit based on your ex-spouse's record. Your ex-spouse doesn't need to know you're claiming, and it doesn't reduce their benefit amount. You can claim the higher of your own retirement benefit or the divorced spousal benefit.

Tax Implications of Social Security Income

Many retirees are surprised to learn that social security benefits can be subject to income tax. The IRS uses a formula based on your combined income (also called provisional income) to determine how much of your benefit is taxable.

Your provisional income equals your adjusted gross income plus any nontaxable interest plus half of your social security income. Here's how the thresholds break down:

How Social Security Fits into Your Broader Retirement Plan

Social security claiming strategies don't exist in isolation. They interact with your retirement savings, investment strategies, Medicare coverage, and overall financial plan. Here's what plan sponsors and participants should consider:

Coordinate with Retirement Plan Distributions

The timing of 401(k) or IRA withdrawals affects your taxable income, which affects how much of your social security benefits get taxed. Delaying Social Security while drawing down taxable accounts in your early 60s can create a lower-tax window and preserve more of your lifetime benefits.

Factor in Medicare Part B Premiums

Medicare coverage begins at age 65, and your Part B premiums are income-tested. Higher income (including social security income and retirement account withdrawals) can trigger Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amounts (IRMAA), increasing your Medicare Part B costs. Planning your claiming age alongside your Medicare enrollment helps minimize this financial burden.

Consider Your Family History and Health

Your life expectancy and family history of longevity should influence when you claim. If you have reason to expect a shorter life expectancy, claiming earlier may make sense. But if your family members have historically lived into their 80s and 90s, delaying benefits provides more money over your lifetime and stronger protection against outliving your savings.

Account for Dependent Children

If you have dependent children under age 18 (or up to 19 if still in high school), they may qualify for benefits based on your record when you start receiving benefits. This can influence your claiming timeline, particularly for parents who had children later in life.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the earliest I can claim Social Security?

You can start your social security retirement benefits as early as age 62. However, if you claim your benefit early, it permanently reduces your monthly benefit by up to 30% compared to waiting until your full retirement age. When you reach your full retirement age, you receive 100% of your PIA. The decision depends on your health, financial needs, marital status, and other income sources.

What are delayed retirement credits?

For each month you delay claiming past your full retirement age (up to age 70), your benefit increases by approximately 0.67% per month, or 8% per year. These delayed retirement credits result in a permanently higher benefit for the rest of your life.

Can my spouse claims benefits on my record?

Yes. A spouse can receive up to 50% of your primary insurance amount as a spousal benefit, provided they're at least 62 or caring for your child under 16. If your spouse has their own work record, they'll receive the higher benefit of the two.

How does claiming early affect my surviving spouse?

If you claim early and receive a reduced benefit, your surviving spouse receives that reduced amount (assuming it's still the higher of the two household benefits). Delaying your claim locks in a higher benefit that your surviving spouse receives after you pass away.

Will working while receiving benefits reduce my social security payments?

If you haven't reached your full retirement age, the annual earnings limit applies. In 2026, the limit is $24,480 for those under FRA (benefits reduced by $1 for every $2 over the limit). Once you reach full retirement age, there's no earnings limit and no reduction.

Is Social Security income taxable?

Yes, depending on your combined income. For individuals with combined income above $25,000, up to 50% of benefits may be taxable. Above $34,000, up to 85% becomes taxable. For married couples, the thresholds are $32,000 and $44,000 respectively.

What is the maximum Social Security benefit?

The maximum monthly benefit in 2026 for someone claiming at age 70 is $5,251. At full retirement age it's approximately $4,018, and at age 62 it's $2,969. Your actual benefit depends on your earnings history and when you claim social security benefits.

Should I factor investment advice into my claiming decision?

Absolutely. Your broader investment strategies, including how you draw from retirement savings and taxable brokerage accounts, directly impact when it makes sense to start benefits. A coordinated approach that considers Social Security alongside your complete financial picture typically produces better outcomes.

How does the Bipartisan Budget Act affect my options?

The Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015 eliminated the "file and suspend" and "restricted application" strategies. Under current rules, if you file for benefits before your FRA, you're deemed to be filing for all benefits you're eligible for. Survivor benefits remain a separate filing decision.
