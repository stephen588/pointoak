Frequently Asked Questions

How often should you conduct a 401(k) recordkeeper RFP?

The Department of Labor presumes plan sponsors conduct RFPs every three to five years as part of prudent fiduciary practice. We recommend annual benchmarking to monitor fees and services, with a comprehensive recordkeeper RFP every three years. However, you should conduct an RFP sooner if your recordkeeper is acquired, you experience service problems, or your plan experiences 20%+ growth in assets or participants. Court cases like George v. Kraft Foods demonstrate that failing to conduct regular searches creates fiduciary litigation risk for plan fiduciaries responsible for retirement plan oversight.

What are the key criteria for evaluating recordkeeper proposals?

The most important evaluation criteria include total plan cost across all fee types, technology capabilities for participant and sponsor portals, service quality measured through response times and dedicated support, SECURE 2.0 implementation readiness for mandatory and optional provisions, and cybersecurity controls to protect participant data. We also evaluate recordkeeper financial stability, investment options and investment advice availability, transition support capabilities, and the quality of participant services including education resources. Our scoring framework weighs each criterion based on your plan's specific objectives, ensuring you evaluate proposals on what matters most to your organization.

How many recordkeepers should you include in an RFP?

We typically recommend including 3-5 potential recordkeepers in your request for proposal process. This provides meaningful comparison without creating evaluation overwhelm. The specific number depends on your plan size and complexity. Smaller plans may focus on 3 providers specializing in that market segment, while larger plans might evaluate 4-5 to ensure comprehensive market coverage. We help identify which recordkeepers are best suited to your plan characteristics, so you're comparing qualified vendors on a level playing field rather than wasting time on providers who don't serve your market or lead to confusion.

What is the difference between benchmarking and an RFP?

Benchmarking compares your current fees and services against industry standards to determine reasonableness, typically using existing plan data and market surveys to make sense of your costs. A recordkeeper search through an RFP is a comprehensive process where you solicit detailed proposals from different recordkeepers, evaluate their complete service offerings, and potentially switch providers. Think of benchmarking as an annual health check, while an RFP is a full market evaluation. We recommend benchmarking annually and conducting a formal recordkeeper RFP every three to five years to ensure plan sponsors meet compliance requirements.

Do you need an advisor to conduct a recordkeeper RFP?

While you can technically conduct an RFP yourself, the complexity and fiduciary implications make retirement plan advisor support essential for most plan sponsors. A specialized firm brings market knowledge of 130+ recordkeepers, expertise in drafting comprehensive RFP documents, objectivity in evaluating rfp responses, and documentation practices that protect you in audits or litigation. More importantly, working with an advisor who acts as a named 3(21) or 3(38) fiduciary shifts legal responsibility for the process and demonstrates prudent expert reliance. The cost of advisor support is typically far less than the fee savings and liability protection it provides to assist your organization.

Is benchmarking alone enough, or do I need a full RFP?

Benchmarking provides valuable fee and service comparisons, but it doesn't create the same level of fiduciary documentation as a formal request for proposal. If you're satisfied with your recordkeeper and benchmarking shows your fees are competitive, you may not need an immediate RFP. However, DOL guidance and court precedents suggest plan sponsors should conduct comprehensive RFPs every three to five years regardless of satisfaction levels. We help you determine whether a simple benchmark, a request for information from your current provider, or a full recordkeeper search makes sense based on how long it's been since your last formal evaluation and what the market data reveals about the services provided.

Our recordkeeper was just acquired - do we need to do a new RFP?

When your recordkeeper is acquired, you should evaluate whether to conduct an RFP within 6-9 months of the announcement to discuss your concerns. The key question is whether you're now with the acquirer or acquiree. If your provider was acquired by another company, verify that the acquisition won't impact service quality, pricing, platform capabilities, or the investment options and participant services your plan offers. If your provider acquired another firm, ensure they can successfully integrate without service disruption. You didn't hire the new entity, so conducting a recordkeeper search confirms the combined organization still meets your needs and compliance requirements.
