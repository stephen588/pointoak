Is an Investment Policy Statement required for a 401(k) plan?

ERISA does not legally require an IPS. However, the DOL stated in Interpretive Bulletin 2016-01 that an IPS serves a "legitimate purpose" and is consistent with ERISA's duty of prudence. The DOL regularly requests IPSs during plan audits to evaluate how plan sponsors manage fiduciary risk. With 96% of large plans maintaining an IPS, not having one subjects your retirement plan to greater scrutiny and potential breach allegations.

What should be included in a retirement plan Investment Policy Statement?

A comprehensive IPS defines investment objectives, outlines Investment Committee roles and fiduciary responsibilities, establishes asset allocation guidelines and permitted asset classes, specifies criteria for selecting investment options including share class selection, describes monitoring procedures on a regular basis, and documents the review process. It should include both quantitative factors like expense ratios and performance benchmarks, and qualitative factors like manager tenure and organizational stability. An effective IPS must balance specificity with flexibility to serve as a practical road map.

How often should an Investment Policy Statement be updated?

Review your IPS annually on a regular basis to ensure it remains current with regulations, investment products, and plan demographics. Make amendments as needed for material changes in plan design, investment philosophy, or fiduciary roles. Conduct comprehensive rewrites every three to five years to refresh the entire document and confirm it reflects current thinking about investment goals, the time horizon for plan participants, and market conditions affecting how assets are invested.

What is the difference between 3(21) and 3(38) fiduciaries in an IPS?

A 3(21) co-fiduciary provides investment recommendations and advice while retirement plan sponsors retain final decision-making authority and accountability. A 3(38) investment manager has discretionary authority to select and replace the plan's investments without sponsor approval. Your IPS must clearly define which role your investment adviser serves, as this affects your liability exposure, documentation requirements, and Investment Committee responsibilities for managing fiduciary risk.

Can having an IPS protect plan sponsors from fiduciary lawsuits?

An IPS demonstrates procedural prudence by documenting your decision-making framework and evaluation criteria. ERISA doesn't require perfect investment outcomes, only prudent processes for making investment decisions. A well-constructed IPS that you actually follow can protect against breach allegations even when investments underperform in volatile markets. However, having an IPS you don't follow creates greater fiduciary risk than having no IPS at all.

Our advisor gave us a template IPS but we're worried it's too generic. Should we customize it or is a template good enough?

Generic templates create the "check the box" problem that exposes retirement plan sponsors to greater risk. Your IPS should reflect your actual decision-making process, participant demographics, investment philosophy, and the specific investment options available to plan participants. A template that doesn't match how you operate becomes evidence of fiduciary failure during audits. Customization based on your plan's specific characteristics, investment goals, and employee needs is essential for meaningful protection and represents best practice.

We've had the same IPS for eight years and never updated it. Are we in trouble with the DOL?

A stale IPS creates compliance risk but doesn't automatically trigger DOL enforcement. Schedule an immediate review to identify outdated provisions, missing regulatory updates affecting investment managers, and disconnects between your IPS and actual practice. Document the evaluation process and any amendments. Going forward, establish annual review procedures on a regular basis to prevent future staleness.
