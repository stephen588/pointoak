How We Help

Understanding ERISA's "reasonable" standard

ERISA requires 401(k) fees to be reasonable but doesn't define it. Plan fiduciaries lack objective criteria for evaluating whether they're paying excessive fees for services provided to the plan.

We apply a documented benchmarking process using 450+ data points to establish what's reasonable for your plan size, creating defensible proof of prudent decision-making that protects you from fiduciary liability.

Decoding 408(b)(2) fee disclosures

Fee information in disclosures lacks standardized formats and often appears intentionally confusing. Hidden costs like revenue sharing are buried in expense ratios, making true cost comparison impossible for many plans.

Our Live Bid methodology calculates all-in plan costs (direct plus indirect) for accurate provider comparison, eliminating confusion about what plan participants actually pay from their account balances.

Identifying asset-based fees

Many service providers charge administration fees as a percentage of plan assets. As assets invested grow, you pay more for the same recordkeeping work, creating unfair cost escalation unrelated to services provided.

We identify asset-based pricing structures and negotiate flat per-participant fees that scale appropriately, ensuring administration costs match the actual work delivered at the plan level.

Small plan fee disadvantage

Small businesses with plans under $5 million in assets pay median all-in fees of 1.89% versus 0.72% for typical plans, yet fiduciary duties remain identical regardless of size or money invested.

We leverage our annual 10,000+ benchmarks to create negotiating power even for smaller plans, helping you access institutional pricing that can substantially reduce high costs through aggregated buying power.

Actively managed vs. index fund expenses

Fund managers of actively managed funds charge higher investment fees than index funds but often don't deliver better retirement outcomes. Plan participants lose money to such fees that could compound over decades.

We evaluate whether actively managed funds in your investment options justify their cost premium or whether low cost index funds would better serve employees' retirement goals.

Missing the 3-year benchmark window

Department of Labor suggests benchmarking every three years minimum, plus annual nondiscrimination testing. Missing this deadline weakens your fiduciary defense if high fees are later challenged as excessive during an audit.

Annual benchmarking keeps you ahead of problems. Our process documentation shows continuous monitoring of investment management fees and plan expenses, strengthening your position far beyond minimum compliance requirements.

What are 408(b)(2) fee disclosures and why do they matter to plan sponsors?

408(b)(2) disclosures are Department of Labor-required documents that plan service providers must give employer sponsors, detailing all fees paid both directly and indirectly from plan assets. They're critical for evaluating fee reasonableness and protecting against fiduciary liability, but they lack standardized formats and often hide indirect costs like revenue sharing in complex tables. We translate these disclosures into clear comparisons, calculating your true all-in plan costs for accurate benchmarking.

Can plan sponsors be held personally liable for excessive 401(k) fees?

Yes. Under ERISA, employer sponsors are plan fiduciaries who must ensure fees charged are reasonable for services received. Courts have found plan sponsors personally liable when they fail to benchmark plan fees regularly or pay costs substantially above market rates. As Yale Law School research confirms, fee litigation has expanded from large corporations to mid-sized companies and small businesses. Documentation of a prudent benchmarking process is your primary defense against such liability.

What is revenue sharing in 401(k) plans and how does it affect what participants pay?

Revenue sharing occurs when mutual funds rebate a portion of their expense ratios to recordkeepers or advisors to offset plan costs. While not inherently bad, it creates conflicts of interest that can keep investment fees higher than necessary. Revenue sharing is an indirect annual fee paid from investment returns rather than directly from plan assets, making true costs less transparent to plan participants. We identify all revenue sharing in our benchmarking to calculate your all-in plan expenses.

How do small businesses compete with larger plans on 401(k) fees?

Small businesses face a disadvantage because service providers often charge asset based fees that don't reflect economies of scale for plan investments. However, you can still access cost effective pricing through several strategies: requesting flat per-participant pricing instead of asset-based administration fees, choosing low cost index funds over high cost actively managed funds, and working with investment advisory services that aggregate buying power across many plans. We perform over 10,000 benchmarks annually, giving small businesses negotiating leverage even with modest assets invested, helping employees achieve better retirement outcomes without excessive fees draining their money and interest growth.
