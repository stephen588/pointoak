Fiduciary Services
What is a fiduciary, and why does it matter for my retirement plan?
A fiduciary is someone who is legally obligated to act in the best interests of your plan's participants. Under ERISA, plan sponsors have fiduciary duties that carry personal liability. If you're not meeting those obligations, you and your organization could face penalties, lawsuits, or DOL enforcement actions.
That's where we come in. PointOak's advisors are registered and act as named fiduciaries under ERISA, helping to protect you from liability while making sure your plan stays compliant. You can explore our full approach on our Fiduciary Support page.
What is the difference between a 3(21) and 3(38) fiduciary?
These are two distinct fiduciary roles defined under ERISA:
         * 3(21) Co-Fiduciary: We provide recommendations and guidance on investment decisions, but you (the plan sponsor) retain final decision-making authority.
         * 3(38) Investment Manager: We take on discretionary authority for selecting and monitoring your plan's investments, assuming direct responsibility for those decisions.
Both roles provide meaningful liability protection. The right fit depends on how much involvement you want in the investment decision-making process. We work with you to determine which structure best suits your plan's needs.
What is a Virtual 401(k) Fiduciary File?
It's a digital documentation system we maintain for every client. We document all processes, procedures, meetings, and decisions related to your retirement plan. If you're ever audited by the DOL, this file serves as your paper trail, showing that you followed a prudent, well-documented process in managing your plan.

Investment Management
How does PointOak evaluate investments?
We use a proprietary 10-point pass/fail scoring system that evaluates investment managers across multiple factors, including fund style, risk-adjusted returns, peer group rankings, fund manager tenure, and expense ratios. Our methodology is used to evaluate over $120 billion in retirement plan assets every quarter.
We conduct quarterly investment monitoring and provide clear, actionable reports so you and your Investment Committee can make informed decisions. Visit our Investment Analysis page for a closer look at how this process works.
What is an Investment Policy Statement (IPS)?
An IPS is a written document that outlines your plan's investment objectives, guidelines, and the criteria used to select and monitor investments. Think of it as the rulebook for your plan's investment decisions. It helps your Investment Committee stay disciplined and provides documentation that supports your fiduciary obligations.
We help draft and maintain your IPS as part of our investment analysis services.
What are target date funds, and how do you evaluate them?
Target date funds (TDFs) are investment options that automatically adjust their asset allocation as participants approach retirement age. They've become the most common default investment option in retirement plans, but not all TDFs are built the same.
Our TDF consulting process includes a multi-step suitability and selection analysis. We evaluate participant demographics, compare glide path strategies ("to" retirement vs. "through" retirement), review fund fees and share classes, and assess historical performance. The goal is to make sure your TDF selection is truly aligned with your participants' characteristics. Learn more on our Target Date Fund Consulting page.

Fees and Benchmarking
How often should we benchmark our retirement plan?
The DOL requires that plans be benchmarked at least every three years to determine reasonableness of fees. However, we recommend annual benchmarking to stay ahead of market changes and maintain leverage with your current provider.
What is PointOak's benchmarking process?
We conduct custom "Live Bid" benchmarking studies that use your actual plan information rather than average plan demographics. Each study reviews over 450 data points per bidder across three key areas: fees, investments, and services. This approach gives you a far more accurate picture of how your plan stacks up than standard industry benchmarks.
The results give you the ammunition to renegotiate better fees or services with your current provider, or to make a well-informed provider switch if needed. Visit our Provider & Fee Benchmarking page for details.
How do I know if my plan's fees are reasonable?
That's a question more plan sponsors should be asking. The truth is, it's hard to know without a proper benchmark. Fees can be buried across multiple layers (recordkeeper fees, investment expenses, advisory fees, and more), and what looks competitive on the surface may not hold up under scrutiny.
We help you uncover inefficiencies and compare your total plan costs against similar-sized plans in your market. Our goal is that you never pay more than you should.
