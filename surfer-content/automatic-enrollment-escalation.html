Frequently Asked Questions

Is automatic enrollment in 401k mandatory for all plans?

No, mandatory automatic enrollment applies only to new 401 k plans and 403(b) plans established after December 29, 2022, for plan years beginning after December 31, 2024. Plans established prior are not subject to the mandate. Exemptions exist for governmental plans, government plans, church plans, businesses with fewer employees (under 10), and businesses in existence less than three years. Multiple employer plans and single employer plans established before the effective date are grandfathered.

What are the requirements for automatic enrollment 401k compliance?

SECURE Act 2.0 requires plans to use default contribution rates between 3% and 10% of employee's wages for newly eligible employees. Plans must include automatic escalation increasing contributions by 1 percentage point annually until reaching 10-15% of compensation. Required notices must explain the automatic enrollment feature, contribution level options, investment choice in the default investment (typically a balanced fund or target date fund), and how to opt out or withdraw contributions within 90 days.

Can employees opt out of automatic enrollment 401k plans?

Yes, employees always retain the right to opt out of participation or change their contribution rate at any time. The automatic enrollment feature cannot require employees to participate against their wishes. Plans must provide clear information about how eligible employees can opt out, and the process must be straightforward. Under eligible automatic contribution arrangements, participants can withdraw automatically enrolled contributions within 90 days without the early withdrawal penalties that normally apply to retirement accounts.

What is the difference between EACA and QACA structures?

An eligible automatic contribution arrangement (EACA) requires specific required notices, allows 90-day permissible withdrawals, and provides safe harbor correction procedures. A qualified automatic contribution arrangement (QACA) includes EACA features plus requires employer contributions through matching (100% match on first 1% of pay and 50% on next 5%) or 3% nonelective contribution to all eligible employees. QACA plans receive nondiscrimination testing relief for highly compensated employees and non highly compensated employees.

What happens if you don't implement automatic enrollment when required?

Plans that fail to implement mandatory automatic enrollment face DOL penalties and required corrective contributions to affected employees. The employer's plan may lose qualified status, triggering tax consequences. Corrective contributions must make participants whole for missed retirement savings opportunities, and the dollar amount could be substantial if multiple employees were excluded. Enhanced correction procedures under the Internal Revenue Code offer some relief, but proper implementation is preferable.

How do we correct automatic enrollment failures without massive penalties?

SECURE 2.0 enhanced safe harbor correction procedures. For reasonable errors, employers correct by allocating contributions to affected employees based on what they would have received at the default contribution rate with applicable matching contributions or nonelective contributions. The correction methodology follows specific rules in final regulations. Prompt correction reduces penalties significantly. We guide calculations and documentation demonstrating reasonable efforts.

Can we change our default contribution rate after implementing automatic enrollment?

Yes, plan sponsors can amend plans to change default rates for future enrollments. Changes typically apply only to new hires enrolled after the amendment's effective date. Existing participants automatically enrolled at previous rates continue unless you conduct re-enrollment resetting all participants to new defaults. Re-enrollment requires participant communications and compliance with contribution requirements and eligibility requirements.

What is the best default contribution rate for maximizing participation and retirement readiness?

Research shows 60% of plans use defaults of 4% or higher. While 3% satisfies minimum contribution requirements, it may leave employees undersaved. Setting rates too high triggers opt-outs. Optimal rates depend on workforce demographics, compensation levels, and employer matching contribution structures. Plans with generous matches sustain higher defaults. We analyze your employee population recommending rates balancing participation with adequacy.

How does automatic enrollment impact highly compensated employees and testing?

Automatic enrollment improves nondiscrimination testing by increasing participation rates among non highly compensated employees. Higher NHCE participation allows highly compensated employees to contribute more without violating actual deferral percentage tests. Qualified automatic contribution arrangements receive even greater testing relief satisfying safe harbor requirements exempting them from ADP and ACP testing. We help you understand how your structure affects testing and ensure you comply with all rules.
