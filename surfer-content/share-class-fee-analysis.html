C shares with ongoing 12b-1 fees

Mutual funds sold as "no load funds" still charge C shares' ongoing 12b-1 fees of 1% annually, which exceed the initial investment cost of A shares' front end sales charge over a certain period.

12b-1 fees and sub-TA fees pay recordkeepers and financial advisors, but disclosure documents don't clearly show how these other expenses reduce money available for investing in participant accounts.

Is an R6 share or institutional share better for my retirement plan?

Both typically offer lower costs than retail share classes, but eligibility and pricing vary. R6 shares are specifically designed for employer sponsored retirement plans with $10+ million in assets and eliminate 12b-1 fees entirely. Institutional shares serve large institutional investors broadly and may have different minimums. We compare each fund's expense ratio across all available options, including management fees and other expenses, to determine which share class structure saves the most money for your specific plan and recordkeeper platform.

Best practice involves annual reviews because share class eligibility changes as annual contributions and employer match provisions grow your plan's assets held. When you cross $10 million, $50 million, or $100 million, new institutional share classes with lower fees typically become available. We conduct quarterly monitoring to identify exactly when asset growth triggers opportunities affecting participants' retirement savings.

Warning signs include: participants invested in the same mutual fund at higher retail share classes when institutional options exist, any fund's expense ratio above 0.5% for index investments or 1.0% for actively managed bond mutual funds, plan assets exceeding $10 million without R6 share access, or no documented review in three-plus years. If individual investors in your plan pay higher annual fees than institutional investors would for the same fund, analysis protects you from fiduciary breach claims and helps participants save money over time.

What is the difference between retail share classes and institutional share classes?

Retail share classes are designed for individual investors making initial investments through financial advisors and typically include sales charges, front end load fees, or ongoing 12b-1 fees. Institutional share classes serve employer sponsored retirement plans and large institutional investors with lower management fees, no sales charges, and minimal other expenses. The same mutual fund offered in both formats can show expense ratio differences of 1% or more annually, which compounds to tens of thousands less in retirement savings over a given period.

What are clean shares and should our employer sponsored retirement plan consider them?

Clean shares strip out all 12b-1 fees and revenue sharing, showing only pure investment management costs with no embedded payments to financial advisors or recordkeepers. They're available to plans typically over $100 million in assets held. Most funds offer clean shares at expense ratios 0.10-0.25% lower than even R6 shares for the same mutual fund. We evaluate whether your plan qualifies, compare total costs including any associated fees, and determine if clean shares better serve your participants' financial goals.
