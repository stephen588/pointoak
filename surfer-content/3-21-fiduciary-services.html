What is the difference between a 3(21) and 3(38) fiduciary?

A 3(21) investment advisor is a co-fiduciary who provides investment recommendations, but you retain final authority over investment decisions. A 3(38) investment manager has full discretion to select and replace investments without employer approval. The 3(21) costs less and maintains your control, while the 3(38) fiduciary offers maximum liability transfer.

Do I need a 3(21) or 3(38) fiduciary for my 401(k)?

It depends on your preference for control versus delegation of discretionary authority. Choose 3(21) if you want expert investment advice while maintaining final authority over the plan's investments. Choose 3(38) if you lack time or expertise managing plan assets and prefer to delegate investment decisions entirely to an investment manager.

Can a 3(21) fiduciary help reduce my liability?

Yes. As a named fiduciary under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act, we share fiduciary responsibilities and render investment advice you can rely upon. While you retain final authority, courts recognize that acting on prudent 3(21) investment recommendations significantly reduces business owner personal liability exposure.

How often should I benchmark my 401(k) plan?

ERISA requires benchmarking at least every three years to determine service provider fee reasonableness. We recommend annual benchmarking because plan providers often raise administration costs incrementally. Our Live Bid process reviews recordkeeper, investment manager, and TPA compensation to ensure you never pay more than necessary.

Are all financial advisors fiduciaries?

No. Many are broker-dealers held only to a 'suitability' standard, meaning they must recommend appropriate investment options but not necessarily the lowest-cost or best choices serving participants' best interests. Only registered investment advisors are legally required to act as investment fiduciaries. Always ask if your financial advisor is a fiduciary 100% of the time.

What price range should we expect for a 3(21) fiduciary advisor - flat fee or AUM?

Most 3(21) investment advisers charge 0.25%-1.00% of plan's assets (AUM basis), which aligns advisor and plan growth. Flat fees range from $7,500-$55,000 annually depending on plan complexity and administration requirements. For plans under $1 million, flat fee compensation may offer better value. We provide both options and guidance on which serves your best interests.

How do we balance participant freedom with our fiduciary duty?

Under ERISA Section 404(c), you can limit liability for participant investment decisions by offering a qualified default investment alternative and prudent fund lineup. We help you provide appropriate investment options while restricting high-risk choices that could trigger fiduciary concerns. Our oversight ensures you meet duties while giving employees reasonable control.
