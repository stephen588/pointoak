What is the difference between a 3(21) and 3(38) fiduciary?

A 3(21) fiduciary is a co-fiduciary who provides investment advice and recommendations, but you retain final decision-making authority and some liability. A 3(38) investment manager assumes full discretionary authority over investment selection and monitoring, taking on that liability themselves. The 3(38) role provides maximum fiduciary protection for plan sponsors who want to transfer investment responsibility entirely.

What happens during a DOL audit of our retirement plan?

The Department of Labor's Employee Benefits Security Administration may audit your plan to review fiduciary compliance, investment selections, fee arrangements, and participant disclosures. They closed 907 civil investigations in 2022, with 595 leading to monetary results. We act as your point of contact with the DOL, provide all required documentation from your Virtual 401(k) Fiduciary Files, and guide you through the process.

Can we delegate all fiduciary responsibilities to avoid liability?

You can transfer significant fiduciary liability by hiring a 3(38) investment manager for discretionary investment management and a 3(16) plan administrator for plan administration duties. However, plan sponsors retain some fiduciary responsibility for prudently selecting and monitoring these service providers. The key is creating a documented, prudent process for oversight that demonstrates you acted in participants' best interests.

What are the biggest fiduciary compliance mistakes plan sponsors make?

The most common mistakes include failing to benchmark fees every three years as required, lacking proper documentation of fiduciary decisions, not monitoring investments quarterly, accepting advice from providers with conflicts of interest, and missing required participant disclosures. Many employers also don't realize they have functional fiduciary status even if not named in plan documents, creating unexpected personal liability exposure.

The DOL issued cybersecurity guidance in 2021 and now includes cybersecurity questions in retirement plan investigations. Plan fiduciaries have an obligation to protect participant data and ensure service providers have appropriate safeguards.
