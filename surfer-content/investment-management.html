How Investment Management Works

A prudent process starts with an Investment Policy Statement defining asset allocation targets, risk tolerance thresholds, and watchlist triggers for your investment portfolios. Each fund is then measured quarterly across asset classes including mutual funds, exchange traded funds, fixed income, and private equity alternatives, using benchmarks for portfolio performance, fund managers' tenure, and expense ratios. That sequence turns portfolio management into a documentable record a DOL auditor can follow.

PointOak's proprietary 10-point pass/fail system evaluates fund valuations, risk-adjusted returns, and share class suitability against IPS criteria every quarter. In 2024, 65% of active large-cap U.S. equity investment managers failed to achieve above average fund performance relative to the S&P 500 per SPIVA, which is why disciplined money management criteria matter more than chasing returns.

3(38) vs. 3(21): Which Structure Fits Your Plan?

Both are ERISA-recognized investment management structures. The right choice depends on your governance capacity and how much decision authority your management teams want to retain.

Factor

3(38) Discretionary Manager

3(21) Co-Fiduciary Advisor

Decision Authority

Sponsor retains final approval

Higher; committee reviews each change

Residual Sponsor Duty

Choose 3(38) when your committee wants lower governance workload and clearer accountability lines. Choose 3(21) when leadership wants final approval on every change. Either way, DOL guidance requires prudent selection and monitoring of your investment adviser across shifting capital markets and market valuations. PointOak serves as a named fiduciary under both structures and can evaluate passive management exposure, active fund selection, and separately managed account options.

Who Needs Investment Management?

No documented monitoring process. Your plan has no IPS, no scoring criteria, and no written committee record. That is exactly the financial complexity that draws DOL attention during an audit.

TDF used as QDIA without recent review. Target date funds require ongoing evaluation of glide paths and fees per DOL guidance. A fund chosen once and never revisited is not a prudent process.

Governance fatigue. Your committee feels unequipped to examine internal systems and evaluate fund-specific data. A 3(38) structure with quarterly monitoring reduces that burden while maintaining the governance record DOL requires.

When it may not be the right fit. If your organization already employs internal investment staff who maintain a documented IPS and run quarterly reviews, a provider and fee benchmarking study may be more appropriate.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a 3(21) and a 3(38) investment manager?

Voice Answer: A 3(21) co-fiduciary advises your committee while you retain final fund approval; a 3(38) investment manager assumes full discretionary responsibility for selecting and monitoring your plan's investments. Under 3(21), PointOak provides recommendations and your committee approves each fund change; under 3(38), PointOak makes those decisions directly. DOL guidance requires sponsors to prudently select and monitor the investment adviser under either arrangement, and a 3(38) must be a bank, insurance company, or registered investment adviser. PointOak offers both structures.

Does hiring a 3(38) eliminate fiduciary liability?

Voice Answer: No. Hiring a 3(38) reduces exposure for individual fund decisions but does not eliminate your obligation to prudently select and monitor the manager. DOL guidance states that even when you delegate investment decisions to a qualified investment adviser, you retain the duty to monitor. That means documenting selection criteria, reviewing credentials through FINRA BrokerCheck, and maintaining a fiduciary file with evidence of ongoing oversight. PointOak's Virtual 401(k) Fiduciary File supports that residual obligation directly.

Do target date funds require ongoing monitoring?

Voice Answer: Yes. DOL guidance covers TDF selection and monitoring, including glide path evaluation, underlying asset class review, and expense ratio benchmarking. In 2022, TDFs represented 38% of 401(k) plan assets per ICI, making QDIA oversight consequential for most sponsors. A fund chosen as the plan's QDIA still requires periodic re-evaluation to confirm it remains appropriate for participant demographics and risk tolerance. PointOak's TDF consulting follows DOL guidelines and documents every evaluation in your fiduciary file.

How do we verify an investment manager's credentials?

Voice Answer: Use FINRA BrokerCheck and the SEC's Investment Adviser Public Disclosure database to verify registration, credentials, disciplinary history, and disclosed conflicts of interest. FINRA (the financial industry regulatory authority) makes BrokerCheck free and publicly accessible, drawing from the CRD and IARD systems. For registered investment advisers in the securities industry, SEC IAPD includes Form ADV filings with fee disclosures and regulatory constraints covering client service standards.

Why do plan fees and share classes matter?

Voice Answer: In 2024, the asset-weighted average equity fund expense ratio was 0.40% per ICI; share class and fee differences are directly linked to long-term participant outcomes. Fee drag compounds over time and is one of the clearest places to demonstrate fiduciary prudence. In 2024, 92% of long-term mutual fund gross sales went to no-load funds without 12b-1 fees per ICI, reflecting how financial markets have shifted. PointOak's share class review and Live Bid benchmarking assist clients in identifying overcharges directly linked to participants' financial future and tax strategy around retirement savings.
