What's the difference between a 3(21) and 3(38) fiduciary?

A 3(21) co-fiduciary provides investment recommendations and guidance while you retain final decision-making authority and fiduciary responsibility for plan investments and managing plan assets. A 3(38) investment manager assumes full discretionary responsibility for selecting, managing, monitoring, and benchmarking the investment offerings available in your retirement plans. With a 3(38), we take on the investment fiduciary liability under ERISA law, providing you with the highest level of protection for investment-related decisions while acting solely in the best interests of plan participants.

What are the most common ERISA violations that result in DOL penalties?

Common violations include: failing to file Form 5500 and required forms on time (daily penalties up to $2,670 from Department of Labor and $250 from IRS), not providing Summary Plan Descriptions or Summary of Material Modifications within required timeframes (up to $110 per day per request), late deposit of employee deferrals (must be deposited as soon as reasonably segregated from general business assets), failure to follow plan document terms, inadequate investment monitoring and analysis of plan assets, paying unreasonable fees from the plan, and HIPAA violations for group health plans. The best defense is a documented, prudent process maintained by qualified fiduciaries aware of all ERISA mandates.

Can we outsource ERISA compliance completely or do we need legal counsel?

While you can delegate significant fiduciary responsibilities by hiring a 3(16) plan administrator and 3(38) investment manager for retirement plans and other employee benefit plans, the plan sponsor retains ultimate responsibility for selecting and monitoring service providers under ERISA law. This is a fiduciary act in itself under the Income Security Act ERISA. However, by working with qualified fiduciaries like PointOak who assume named fiduciary status, you significantly reduce your liability and risk exposure. We help you meet your fiduciary obligations with confidence. For specific legal questions about the Internal Revenue Code or complex compliance issues, we recommend working with ERISA legal counsel alongside our services.

How does ERISA compliance relate to SECURE 2.0 Act provisions?

The SECURE 2.0 Act introduced numerous provisions affecting ERISA compliance for retirement plans and employee benefit plans, with mandatory and optional features rolling out through 2026. Mandatory provisions include updated participant count methodology for determining who is subject to audit requirements, required distributions for natural disasters (if adopted), and various disclosure requirements to employees and beneficiaries. Plan sponsors must stay current on which provisions are required versus optional and ensure plan documents and wrap documents are amended accordingly. We monitor all regulatory changes and help you implement best practices to maintain compliance with both ERISA and SECURE 2.0 requirements, protecting your company and your program.
